Drowning in the Record

⬦   ⬦   ⬦

Out along a farm road, a grain silo towers with the confidence of a culture that believes it has solved the problem of keeping. Inside its metal embrace, grain takes on its own physics. It settles, shifts, crusts over, hollows beneath itself, and when the surface hardens into the illusion of ground, a body can step forward believing it has found footing only to be taken under by the very abundance meant to sustain life.

The archive works the same way. This is a game about learning to read the surface before you step.

How to Play

You are not here to find the answer. You are here to evaluate the record.

Before you begin

  • Choose a role. Each one asks a different first question. You cannot change it once you begin.
  • Read everything before you respond. The documents are doing something. Your job is to figure out what.
  • There are no trick questions, but there are questions without clean answers. That is intentional.
  • All your responses are saved locally and logged to the archive ledger.

The five rooms

PrologueRead the exhibit brief. Click the three bold phrases. Answer three intake questions. Save before you proceed.
Room ITwo official documents. Match each euphemism to the plain-language term it is hiding. Correct matches unlock a witness testimony.
Room IIFive sources arrive. Rate each one: does it earn your trust through evidence, or does it only look trustworthy? Rate all five to continue.
Room IIIOne claim appears across eight sources. Trace it back. How many are truly independent? Render your verdict.
Room IVA ranked information feed. Six results. What signals shaped the ranking? Which ones earned their position?
Room VAn AI-generated summary of the full archive. Read it carefully. Complete all seven fields of the Escape Protocol to finish.

What you will produce

Historical claim

What can responsibly be said about what happened, based on the evidence you examined.

Curation claim

What the archive says about its own sources - and whether that claim holds up.

Limitation claim

What cannot be known from this record alone. This is the hardest one. It is also the most important.

Your version

A short account you would stand behind. Specific about what is known. Honest about what is not.

A note on difficulty

Some rooms will feel uncertain. You will not always have enough to be sure. That feeling is accurate - it is not a sign you are doing it wrong. If you get stuck, look for the hint button. If you feel frustrated, the game will recognize that too.

Lesson Plan

A 49-minute lesson for students who are ready to stop analyzing sources and start analyzing the system that selected them.

This lesson fits inside any APUSH, US History, or Global History unit where students are working with primary source sets. It is designed for one 49-minute block and works as a launch, mid-unit pivot, or capstone reflection. The game replaces the source packet entirely. Students are the analysts. The archive is the object of inquiry.

At a Glance

Placement in unit arc

Best used after students have practiced basic sourcing (HAPP, SOAPS, SOAR) and before or during a DBQ or document-based unit. Also works as a standalone critical media literacy lesson.

Materials needed

Device with browser access for each student. Google Sheets educator URL configured before class. No printed materials required. Optional: sticky notes or exit ticket slips for synthesis.

Ideal class configuration

Individual roles, then whole-class debrief. Assign 2-3 students per role so each role has a small group to compare with during discourse. Works with any class size.

Prior knowledge needed

Students should be able to identify a source, distinguish primary from secondary, and have some familiarity with what a citation is. No prior archival theory required.

Standards Alignment

APUSH Historical Thinking Skills

Argumentation - Construct and evaluate historical arguments. Causation - Analyze why things happened and what resulted. Contextualization - Place sources in broader historical context.

APUSH Key Concept 7.3.I

The federal government used mass incarceration of Japanese Americans during WWII, reflecting the tension between civil liberties and national security claims in wartime.

C3 Framework D2.His.1, D2.His.5

Analyze how historical context shapes the creation and use of sources. Explain how and why perspectives of people have changed over time using multiple sources.

CCSS Literacy RH.11-12.6, 9

Evaluate an author's claims and point of view. Integrate information from diverse sources, noting discrepancies among sources and assessing the reliability of each.

AP Archival Standards AS-1 to AS-6

Read source and system simultaneously. Name what is absent. Trace provenance. Distinguish earned from unearned trust signals. Name the curation decision. State honest limitations.

Civic Literacy

Analyze how media, algorithms, and information systems shape public knowledge. Evaluate the credibility and independence of sources in a digital environment.

Learning Objectives

Students will be able to...

  • Distinguish between source analysis (what a document says) and source-system analysis (why that document is in front of them)
  • Identify earned and unearned trust signals across official documents, news sources, citation chains, and AI-generated summaries
  • Trace how a single institutional claim achieves the appearance of broad corroboration through citation echo
  • Construct a historical claim, a curation claim, and a limitation claim about the same archive
  • Name what cannot be known from a curated record as a form of analytical honesty, not analytical failure

Phase-by-Phase Teacher Guide

Click each phase to expand the teacher guide. BPQ = Breakdown Prompting Question. CFU = Check for Understanding.

Phase 1 Frame - Launch the Question 0 - 5 min ▼
Do NowWrite this prompt on the board before students enter: "You are about to open a source packet about a historical event. Who decided what went in it? Does it matter?" Give students 90 seconds to write. Do not discuss yet.
LaunchCold call 2-3 students. Collect responses without correcting. Then say: "Today you are not going to read a packet. You are going to investigate the system that builds packets like this one. The question is not what happened. The question is: who decided what you would see."
FrameName the distinction explicitly: "We have practiced sourcing - reading what a document says. Today we practice source-system analysis - reading why that document is in front of you." Write both terms on the board. Leave them there.
Opening stamp Every source packet is itself a curation decision. Sourcing tells you what a document says. Source-system analysis tells you why you are reading this document and not a different one.
Phase 2 Assign Roles + Enter the Archive 5 - 8 min ▼
SetupNavigate to the game. Go to mconnor-12.github.io/escapethearchive. Display on projector. Point to the four roles. Read each role question aloud. "These are not just titles. Each role is asking a different first question about the same archive. Your job is to answer that question in every room you enter."
Role assignAssign 2-3 students per role intentionally, not randomly. Suggestion: assign Lead Auditors to students who default to summarizing evidence, Evidence Keepers to students who need practice naming absence, Signal Monitors to students who need support distinguishing authority from appearance, Archive Analysts to students ready for the hardest question.
CFUBefore they click Enter: "What is your role? What question does your role carry into every room? Tell your neighbor." 30 seconds. Circulate and listen. Correct any role confusion before they proceed.
NormSay once: "This is not a race. The game is not testing your speed. It is testing your thinking. Read everything before you respond. Some rooms will feel uncertain. That feeling is accurate."
Phase 3 Independent Work - Prologue Through Room III 8 - 34 min ▼

Students work independently through the Prologue and Rooms I, II, and III. This is the primary work time. Your job is to circulate and confer - not to explain or reduce the difficulty.

Lap 1 (min 8-15) - Check setup

Monitor forStudents completing the three annotation questions in the Prologue with more than one sentence each. Students clicking all three bold phrases. Students who race through without reading - redirect: "Go back to the document. What is the document claiming? Write that before you move."

Lap 2 (min 15-25) - Check conceptual depth

Monitor forStudents in Room I connecting the euphemism to what it hides - not just guessing. Students in Room II making a real judgment about trust signals, not just copying the source description. Students who rate every source "unearned" without reasoning - prompt them: "What would earned look like? Is there a source here that gets closer?"

Lap 3 (min 25-34) - Check Room III claim quality

Monitor forStudents tracing the citation chain rather than just reading the verdict choices. Students naming "echo corroboration" or equivalent concept. Students who pick a verdict without tracing - confer: "Walk me back through the chain. At what point does the evidence stop being independent?"
BPQ for students who are stuck: "You do not need to be certain. Name one thing you can say with confidence, and one thing you cannot determine. Both matter."
BPQ for students who finish early: "Go back to the exhibit brief from the Prologue. Does your answer to Room III change how you read that language? Why or why not?"
BPQ for students who are frustrated: "The game named your frustration intentionally. Why would a game about archives be designed to produce that feeling? What does that teach you?"
Anticipated misconception: sourcing is enough Students will write source analysis (who wrote it, when, why) without making the leap to asking who chose to include this source. Confer: "You have analyzed the source. Now analyze the choice to put this source in front of you. Who made that choice? What else might they have chosen instead?"
Anticipated misconception: more sources = more reliable Room II is specifically designed to surface this. If students are still missing it by the end of Room II: "Count how many of these sources trace back to the same origin. Does the number five mean anything different now?"
Phase 4 Rooms IV - V or Pause Point for Debrief 34 - 44 min ▼

You have two options here depending on pace. Choose based on where most of your class is.

Option AMost students reached Room III or beyond: Let them continue into Room IV (the ranked feed) and begin Room V (AI summary). These rooms move faster than I-III. Students completing Room V will have the full seven-field escape protocol as their exit product.
Option BMost students are in Rooms I-II: Pause the class. Say: "Wherever you are is where you are. Close your laptop. We are going to process what you have already seen." Move directly to Phase 5 discourse. Students complete Rooms IV-V as homework or next class.

If using Option A - monitor Room IV specifically

Key momentThe Densho Digital Archive ranks last in the feed. Watch for students who notice this and ask why. If no one mentions it in debrief, raise it yourself: "The most community-rooted source in this archive ranked last. What does that tell you about what the algorithm is measuring?"
Exemplar student response - Room IV reflection The feed ranked the sponsored content first because it had the most shares and a verified badge. But the verification is for the domain, not the accuracy of the content. The peer-reviewed article had 212 shares and ranked second. Densho had almost no engagement signals but is actually the most community-grounded source in the set. The algorithm ranked what was amplified. It did not rank what was most accountable.
Phase 5 Discourse + Synthesis 44 - 49 min ▼

This is the highest-leverage five minutes of the lesson. Do not rush the synthesis into the last 90 seconds. If needed, cut one to two minutes from Phase 4 to protect this time.

Cross-roleGather one student from each role. Ask: "Your role gave you a different first question. Did you notice different things in the same rooms? What did the Signal Monitor notice that the Lead Auditor might have missed?" Let this run 90 seconds. Do not correct - collect.
Core discourse question 1: "We used the word 'bias' a lot in history class. After today, what is the difference between a biased source and a curated archive? Are they the same problem?"
Core discourse question 2: "The AI summary sounded authoritative and smooth. What did smoothness cost in terms of accuracy? Where did it erase something that mattered?"
Core discourse question 3: "If your teacher built the packet for this unit, what would you want to ask them about how they chose what went in it?"

Exit ticket - choose one

Option 1Write one sentence: what can this archive responsibly claim? Write one sentence: what cannot be claimed from this record alone? (This maps directly to the limitation claim in Room V.)
Option 2Name one thing you noticed about this archive that you would not have noticed before today. Name the specific move you used to notice it.
Option 3For students who completed Room V: their Field 7 ("Your Version") is the exit product. Have them read it back before submitting.
Closing stamp - to say aloud Source analysis asks what a document says. Source-system analysis asks why you are reading it. The limitation claim asks what you cannot know from it. All three are required for honest historical thinking. You practiced all three today.

What to Collect and How to Grade It

From the Google Sheet

The Responses tab in your Archive Ledger captures every open-response text by student and room. The Teacher Grading tab compiles one row per student with all seven escape protocol fields and all room responses. Sort by class period. Grade Field 7 (Your Version) and the Room I role task as your primary written products.

Grading the limitation claim

  • Strong: names a specific type of absence (structural exclusion, accidental loss, institutional decision)
  • Developing: says "we do not have all the information" without specifying what kind of gap
  • Beginning: no limitation offered, or the limit is framed as a gap in student knowledge rather than a gap in the archive

The distinction is whether the student locates the limit in the record, not in themselves.

Grading the historical claim

  • Strong: specific about what happened, attributes the event accurately, names the institutional agent
  • Developing: general claim without attribution or an evidence anchor
  • Beginning: restates what the AI summary said without interrogating it

The best student claims will sound different from the AI summary. That divergence is the evidence of real historical thinking.

Trophies as formative data

The Trophy Log tab shows which precision vocabulary each student used and when. If a student earned "Curation Question" (rare) or "Honest Limit" (rare), their written responses should reflect that thinking. Trophy data is a quick proxy for identifying which students are ready for the next level of source-system analysis.

Extensions and Curriculum Connections

DBQ connection

Before students read a DBQ packet: ask them to write three questions about who assembled it and on what basis. After completing the game, return to those questions. Has their frame changed?

LEQ / argument essay connection

The limitation claim from Room V is a direct model for the complexity requirement in AP argument writing. Students who can name what cannot be argued from a record are stronger claim-makers because they have learned where the claim stops.

Current events connection

After completing the game: give students a recent news event covered by three sources. Ask them to apply the Room III logic. Are these sources independent, or do they trace back to the same institutional origin?

Multi-day version

Day 1: Prologue and Rooms I-II, debrief on source scarcity and saturation. Day 2: Rooms III-IV, debrief on citation echo and algorithmic ranking. Day 3: Room V and full class discussion on the escape protocol as an argument framework.

The Problem

The record arrives in the classroom pre-arranged. By the time students open the packet, someone has already decided what survives, what counts as evidence, and what gets ranked to the top. The problem is not only what is missing from the archive. It is that the abundance itself creates the illusion of completeness.

What students usually learn

How to analyze a source once it is in front of them → sourcing, contextualization, corroboration

What this game teaches

How to interrogate the system that put that source in front of them → curation, saturation, algorithmic trust

What follows is the kind of document I kept wanting when I was the teacher running this activity and could not find it anywhere: a plain account of what students are actually doing in each room, why each room asks what it asks, and how the four roles move through the same material differently. It is written for the person who has twenty minutes to decide whether this fits their unit.

The Pedagogical Argument in One Paragraph

Historical thinking instruction has spent thirty years teaching students to read sources. What it has spent far less time on is teaching students to read the system that assembled those sources in the first place. Who chose what went in the packet? Who ranked one source above another? What gets smoothed over when an algorithm generates the summary? This game moves students through five rooms that are each organized around a different version of that question, from official scarcity through broadcast saturation through algorithmic synthesis. By the time students write their final three claims, they have practiced something that standard sourcing frameworks do not reach: evaluating not the document but the curation decision behind it.

Prologue

The Intake Desk

Students read an official exhibit brief that uses real bureaucratic language from WRA administrative records. They answer three intake questions about what the document claims, what sources it names, and what feels off. Then they click three bold phrases to examine the language more closely.

Student task (10th grade): Read the document. Answer three questions. Save. Then click each bold phrase and see what opens up.

Why this room comes first

Most inquiry activities hand students a packet and ask them to start analyzing. This room asks something earlier: what does the document say about itself before you even begin? Official language has a physics. It smooths, it omits, it presents institutional choices as natural facts. Students need to notice that surface before they can read beneath it. The intake questions are not comprehension checks. They are a first pass at the same move students will make repeatedly across the remaining rooms: what is this record claiming, and is that claim earned?

Room I

The Small Record

Two official documents, three euphemistic phrases, and a drag-and-drop phrase match. Students connect each bureaucratic phrase to the plain-language word it is hiding. Correct answers unlock a witness testimony that the official documents excluded.

Student task: Read both documents. Drag each term to the phrase it matches. Three phrases, three matches, two terms left over. When you get it right, a testimony opens that was not in the official record.

Why this room is designed this way

Euphemism is not decoration. It is a curation decision embedded in official language, and students who can read it are students who can see how archives do their quiet work of naming and renaming. The unlock mechanic is intentional: the witness testimony was excluded from the official record, so students have to earn access to it by first reading the official language carefully enough to name what it is hiding. The role-specific task that follows asks each role to respond to the same two documents from a different first question, giving students who share a classroom a genuine reason to compare what they noticed.

Room II

Many Sources

Five sources, all about the same events, each needing a classification: does this source earn trust, or only look like it does? Students rate all five, then write a pattern analysis.

Student task: Read each source. Press Earned or Unearned for each one. After all five are rated, look across them and answer: what patterns do you notice? What still feels uncertain even with this many sources?

The pedagogical move this room makes

Room I taught scarcity. Room II introduces the opposite problem: abundance. Five sources sounds like a lot, and students often feel more confident here than they should. The room is designed to surface the insight that volume is not the same as independence. Three of the five sources trace back to the same original press release. Students are not told this until they have made their ratings and written their analysis. The reveal, which comes through the archival insight card, should feel like catching something rather than being told something.

Room III

Following the Citation

One claim. Eight citations spanning 1943 to 1989. Students trace the chain back to its source, count how many are truly independent, and render a verdict.

Student task: Read the full citation chain. How many of these sources are actually independent? Trace it back. Then choose your verdict: same source repeated, truly independent sources, or not enough to tell.

What this room is teaching

This is the room about echo corroboration, the mechanism by which a single claim achieves the look of consensus without the substance of it. The citation chain used here is historically accurate: the "cooperation" narrative from WRA records traveled through AP wire, into congressional testimony, into a government history, into textbooks, into encyclopedias, and finally into academic prose that cited "longstanding consensus." Students who trace this chain are practicing the move that distinguishes a skilled reader of secondary sources from a passive one. It is also the room that tends to produce the most visible frustration, which is why the game names that frustration directly and offers a recovery note.

Room IV

The Feed

A ranked information feed with six results. Students rate each one, then write a reflection about what signals shaped their trust and whether those signals were actually earned.

Student task: You are inside a ranked feed. Six results. Rate each one: earned or unearned trust. After all six are rated, write about what drew your attention first and what shaped your ratings.

Why the feed comes fourth, not first

The game moves from official scarcity through managed abundance through citation echo and arrives here at the platform-ranked present. By the time students reach Room IV, they have already traced three other mechanisms by which archives do their curation work. The feed is the contemporary version of the same problem, and it lands differently because students have been building vocabulary for it. The Densho Digital Archive is ranked last. The sponsored content is ranked first. That inversion, and what it means for the knowledge that students can actually reach, is the room's central ethical problem.

Room V

The Summary

An AI-generated summary of the full archive. Students complete a seven-field escape protocol, including a historical claim, a curation claim, a limitation claim, and their own written version of what happened that they would stand behind.

Student task: Read the AI summary. Complete all seven fields. The last one is the most important: write a short account in your own words. Say what happened. Be specific about what we know. Be honest about what we cannot claim.

What the final room is doing and why it ends this way

The AI summary is smooth, coherent, and designed to read like authority. It metabolizes the intercepted letters, the witness testimony, the contradictions, and the gaps into a single polished paragraph that cites 847 items and calls itself balanced. Students who have done the work of the prior four rooms will feel something wrong before they can fully name it. The seven-field protocol is not a quiz on the content. It is a structured practice in what responsible historical claiming looks like when the evidence is incomplete, contested, and curated by someone else. The limitation claim is the one that matters most. Not because students should leave feeling uncertain, but because an answerable limitation is one of the harder civic skills we ask of students, and one of the ones most consistently absent from standard history curriculum.

Using This in Your Classroom

As a standalone activity (1 to 2 class periods)

Assign one role per group or let students choose. Run Prologue through Room II together, then debrief as a class. Rooms III through V work well as homework or a second period. The escape protocol serves as the written exit ticket.

Embedded in a unit (pre or post primary source work)

Works best before or after a primary source packet. Before: primes students to question how the packet was assembled. After: gives language for a metacognitive debrief on what the unit's archive did and did not include.

Tracking student responses

All responses are logged automatically to the Google Sheets archive ledger. The Game Events tab logs every room entry, submission, and completion with a timestamp and role. You can filter by role to compare what Lead Auditors versus Signal Monitors noticed in the same room.

Facilitating a debrief

The three best debrief questions: What did your role let you see that another role might have missed? Where did the AI summary feel most smooth, and what did that smoothness cost? What would a more honest version of the official document have said?

Why this game exists

My students were hungry to debate. They did not want to receive the record. They wanted to challenge it. For a long time, I responded to that hunger by building better packets: more voices, more variety, more careful curation. But somewhere in the process of building a better packet, I started to see the packet itself as the thing I kept stepping around.

What kept surfacing was the gap between sourcing and source-system analysis. Teaching students to evaluate a curated document set is real and necessary work. It builds the ability to read bias, weigh reliability, synthesize evidence. What it rarely touches is the prior question: who assembled this set? On what basis? Which histories were available, which were legible, and which were quietly left on the floor? In a classroom where students are drowning in abundance rather than suffering from scarcity, where a prompt into any AI returns 800 footnoted paragraphs before a student has formed a first question, the most urgent civic skill is not credibility assessment. It is the ability to read the curation decision itself as an argument.

The escape room structure found me before I had fully named the problem. It lets students do both things simultaneously: move through the inquiry and the analysis they are hungry for, while also encountering the system-level decisions embedded in every room. The game does not claim neutrality. It was built by one person, from one classroom, carrying its own curatorial logic. By the end, students have not only interrogated a historical archive. They have interrogated this one, the choices inside it, and the person who made them.
