Our Research

The apparatus, read structurally

Five years of source coding, item analysis, and student cohort data from Dream Charter High School in the Bronx. Methodology, data sources, and analytic decisions are all here.

The New York State Regents in U.S. and Global History asserts a coherent program experienced consistently across the state. The structural evidence shows that promise is partial at best. Inside that bounded reality, teachers have specific, durable space to draw the territory the map cannot show.

This project began in a Bronx classroom where, sitting at students' shoulders for years, the seam between what the exam claimed to measure and what students actually knew began to pull. What the Regents calls historical knowledge is in part a measurement of fluency with one particular map, drawn at one particular scale, by one particular set of cartographers.

Comparability is asserted, not delivered. Consistency is claimed at the institutional level and contradicted at the structural level.

This site presents the four sub-questions that ground the argument, the empirical findings each one surfaces, and the implications for both research and teaching practice. The research behind Re:Source is not advocacy disguised as evidence. It is evidence that, when read clearly, produces an argument about what should change.

Methods

How the corpus was read

Every released source on every Regents administration in USH and Global II from 2022 through January 2026 was coded across four dimensions:

  • Power relation. Whether the source resists, navigates, constructs, maintains, or obscures dominant power.
  • Voice authenticity. Whether the historical actor's words come through as direct, mediated, or composite.
  • Gaze direction. Whether the source looks outward, upward, downward, or inward at the subjects of history.
  • Source attributes. Author identity, perspective, assumed audience, neutrality challenge, and rigor of contextualization.
  • Coefficient of variation on item count, percent multiple choice, and skill emphasis across 17 administrations.
  • Spearman rank correlation on interpretive richness over time.
  • Chi-square pile-up testing on score distributions around the 65 cut score.
  • Paired t-tests on students who took both June and August ELA, testing whether the same student receives the same score on the same skill across different administrations.

468 DCHS students with both performance and demographic data were joined to 49,345 student-by-item responses. Demographic cells were formed by crossing ethnicity, gender, and IEP status. Additional cuts added ELL status, attendance, and housing as stacked vulnerabilities.

  • Cell-level percent correct on identical items. Same content, same rubric, same administration, scored across demographic cells.
  • Resistant-source contestation testing. Items coded Resistant were tested for whether they appear among the 30 most-contested items per administration.
  • Schema-burden analysis. Items requiring outside historical knowledge beyond what the source supplies were tested for differential performance by cell.

NYSED School Quality Reports for 2022-23, 2023-24, and 2024-25 were joined to the DCHS cohort findings to test whether the patterns observed at one school replicate across demographically similar schools statewide. The dataset spans 1,515 school-year observations across 489 schools.

  • Economic Need Index as the primary predictor: r = -0.69 with Global II outcomes citywide.
  • Prior proficiency locking: grade 8 math proficiency r = +0.80 with Regents performance.
  • August retake penalty: 23.8-point gap between June and August sittings at DCHS in the same year, same students.
