About This Project

What does it mean
to be remembered?

An interactive archive experience built at the intersection of historical thinking, memory studies, and the politics of what gets preserved — and what gets left out.

The Creator

Who made Escape the Archive?

D

Dani Connor

Educator · Doctoral Researcher · Digital Humanist

Dani Connor built Escape the Archive as a research project exploring how students engage with archival silences, memory construction, and the politics of historical evidence. Dani is committed to creating learning experiences that go beyond text on a page — where students become detectives, analysts, witnesses, and curators of the past.

This project grew out of questions raised in graduate study of curriculum, memory, and digital pedagogy: What gets into the archive? Who decides? And what happens when we take those decisions seriously?

The Experience

Why should you try it?

Most history instruction hands students a finished record and asks them to interpret it. Escape the Archive does the opposite — it puts you inside the moment when the record is being made, and asks you to grapple with what stays, what disappears, and why.

Whether you're a student, educator, or curious person, the game asks you to think like an archivist, a signal analyst, an evidence keeper. You'll encounter sources that contradict each other, algorithms that shape what you see, and testimony that resists easy categorization.

It's a game. It's an argument. It's an invitation to take the archive seriously.

Your memory is always coming through a frame — and constructed through a frame, whatever that frame is. What if we studied the frame instead of the history itself?

Our Values

What are we committed to?

This project is grounded in a set of principles that shape every design decision — from the sources we include to the roles students play.

🔍

Epistemic Humility

No source is neutral. No archive is complete. We build those truths into the game mechanics themselves — not as footnotes, but as the point.

🧩

Asset-Based Thinking

Students bring real analytical skills to difficult material. This game trusts that and designs for it — every role gives students genuine work to do.

📢

Voice & Representation

Whose voices are in the archive? Who is missing? We ask those questions explicitly, and we design the archive to surface them honestly.

🏛️

Institutional Accountability

Archives are not innocent. This project takes seriously the ways institutions shape what becomes "the record" — and asks students to interrogate that.

♿

Access & Inclusion

The game is designed to work for students across devices and abilities. We are committed to continued accessibility improvements.

🌱

Living Project

The archive grows. We welcome contributions of sources, stories, and feedback from students, educators, and community members.

Multimodal Design

Going beyond just writing

One of the central questions in archival and memory studies is: what forms can evidence take? This project takes that question seriously in its design. You will encounter the archive across multiple modes — not just words on a page.

01

Audio Testimony

Recorded voices, ambient sound, and oral histories that can't be reduced to a transcript.

02

Visual Evidence

Photographs, documents, and images — with all the questions about framing, context, and alteration they carry.

03

Algorithmic Feeds

Simulated signal environments that show how curation shapes what counts as evidence.

04

Interactive Documents

Sources you can annotate, organize, and challenge — not just read passively.

05

Collaborative Analysis

Role-based work that requires students to synthesize evidence across positions and perspectives.

06

Contributed Sources

Real submissions from students, educators, and community members that expand the living archive.

Theoretical Ground

Family, memory, and photographs

This project is informed by scholarship on memory studies, post-memory, and the politics of family archives. A central question running through the design is what Marianne Hirsch calls post-memory — how traumatic histories are carried by subsequent generations not through direct experience, but through the stories and silences of those who lived them.

A good website usually has an about — when it's anonymous, people don't know who you are, why should they trust you? What are you committed to? What is your story, your rationale?

Design Principle, Spring 2026

Photographs are not passive records. As Roland Barthes reminds us, a photograph contains within it the presence of something that has already passed — every image is simultaneously a proof of existence and a marker of absence. This game takes seriously the argument that archives, like family photo albums, are acts of curation, selection, and silence as much as they are acts of preservation.

Think about the photos you didn't take. The stories that weren't told out loud. The documents that were destroyed. Those absences are also part of the archive.

Evidence & Interpretation

What makes it a documentary? What makes it evidence?

?

Is documentary just "real people, their lives, documented in film" — or does it require something more? What is the difference between documentary as evidence and documentary as interpretation?

?

What does it mean to be known — really known — by the people closest to you? How do the people who knew someone best remember them in completely different ways?

?

Every time you retrieve a memory, you make it your own within the narrative you carry. What does that mean for the records we trust as "historical evidence"?

?

Who owns the story? Who gets to tell it? And when those stories enter an archive, what happens to the people who lived them?

?

The chips in the frame are still there. Even as the archive exerts a force, even as it renders warm feelings — the fractures are present. What would it mean to preserve those too?
