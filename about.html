About This Project

What does it mean
for students to shape
the archive itself?

Escape the Archive is an inquiry-based classroom experience built on a provocation: what if students didn't just read primary sources — but became responsible for what counts as evidence in the first place?

The Central Provocation

What if the archive isn't a given?

Most history instruction hands students a finished record and asks them to read it carefully. The archive arrives as a settled thing — a collection of documents, photographs, and artifacts that represent the past. Students learn to analyze what's inside it.

But archives are made, not found. Every document in a collection got there because someone selected it, someone preserved it, someone decided it mattered — and thousands of other records didn't make that cut, or were never created at all.

Escape the Archive asks a different question: what if students didn't just interpret the archive, but participated in the ethical and intellectual work of building it?

Your memory is always coming through a frame — and constructed through a frame, whatever that frame is. What if we studied the frame instead of the history itself?

In-class discussion, Spring 2026

This question sits at the heart of the game. Students aren't passive readers of the archive — they're analysts, witnesses, curators, and signalers who shape what becomes legible as history.

Why It Matters

History education has a selection problem

Historical thinking frameworks in K–12 education almost universally ask students to interrogate sources — to evaluate authorship, corroborate across documents, contextualize within the period. These are real skills. But they take the archive as a starting point rather than as an object of inquiry.

Critical archival theory asks a harder question: not just what does this document tell us, but why does this document exist, who decided to preserve it, what was refused, and what would the record look like if different communities had shaped it?

For students in New York City's public schools — many of whom belong to communities whose histories are underrepresented, distorted, or absent from standard archives — this isn't an abstract theoretical question. It's personal.

01

Selection

Every archive is the product of decisions — what to keep, what to discard, what to digitize, what to leave in a box. Those decisions reflect power.

02

Mediation

Sources don't speak for themselves. They arrive through systems of classification, description, and access that shape what a reader can find and how.

03

Refusal

Silences in the archive aren't accidents. Communities have deliberately withheld records — as protection, as resistance, as sovereignty over their own stories.

04

Agency

Students can be archivists. When young people participate in acts of historical curation, the ethical stakes of the archive become concrete and urgent.

How It Works

Four roles. One archive under pressure.

Escape the Archive is a role-based escape room set inside a fictional archive that is being sorted, searched, and contested in real time. Students take on one of four roles — each with different access to the collection and different responsibilities for what gets preserved.

No single player has the full picture. The game is designed so that students must synthesize evidence across roles — and so that the difficulty of knowing what is true, what is reliable, and what has been deliberately obscured becomes part of the intellectual experience, not an obstacle to it.

🔍

Lead Auditor

Reviews the archive's evidence trail and makes final determinations about what gets included in the official record.

📁

Evidence Keeper

Tracks, organizes, and cross-references the documentary record — surfacing connections others might miss.

📡

Signal Monitor

Watches the information environment for bias, distortion, and manipulation — including algorithmic curation.

👁️

Archive Analyst

Reads across the collection for patterns, gaps, and silences — what the archive says and what it refuses to say.

The Questions Under the Game

What the archive is really asking

Every design decision in Escape the Archive is organized around a set of questions that don't have clean answers — questions that historians, archivists, and communities continue to debate. Students will encounter them not as discussion prompts, but as practical problems they have to navigate.

?

What makes something evidence? Who decides — and what are the stakes of that decision for communities whose records have been systematically destroyed or ignored?

?

What is the difference between a document and a record? Between preservation and curation? Between an archive and a monument?

?

When an algorithm surfaces some sources and buries others, is that neutral? What does it mean to audit the feed rather than just read what's in it?

?

What are the ethics of testimony? When someone tells you something, what do you owe them — as a listener, as a recorder, as someone who will pass that account on?

?

If students help build the archive, what are they responsible for? What does it mean to take the ordinary work of historical inquiry seriously?

Design Commitments

What we're committed to

These aren't values statements — they're constraints that shaped every decision in the game's design, from the roles students play to the sources they encounter.

🧩

Asset-Based Design

Students bring sophisticated analytical thinking to complex material. The game trusts that and is designed for it — every role gives students real intellectual work to do.

🔍

Epistemic Honesty

No source is neutral. No archive is complete. Those truths are built into the mechanics — not mentioned in a handout, but encountered as the structure of the experience.

📢

Voice & Representation

Whose voices are in this archive? Who is absent? These questions are explicit — and the design surfaces them, rather than papering over them.

🌱

Living Project

The archive grows. Contributions from students, educators, and community members are welcome — and the contribution form is built into the site.
